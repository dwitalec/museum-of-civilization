historicalContext: "Launched in November 2010, the Xbox 360 Kinect was Microsoft's bold attempt to reinvent game input entirely — no controller required. Using an RGB camera, infrared depth sensor, and microphone array, it tracked full-body motion and recognized voice commands in real time. Microsoft marketed it with the tagline 'You are the controller,' and the market responded: Kinect sold 10 million units in its first 60 days, earning a Guinness World Record as the fastest-selling consumer electronics device in history. Beyond games, its depth-sensing technology quietly influenced fields far outside living rooms — robotics, accessibility software, medical imaging, and eventually the depth cameras now standard in smartphones. Microsoft discontinued the peripheral in 2017, but the computer vision ideas it mainstreamed never went away."

personalStory: "When I bought the XBox 360, it was my first foray into video games systems outside of Nintendo. The coolest thing for me was the Kinect tracking that allowed you to play games without a controller. In our household, with two young girls, that was dance games. Ultimately this technology was ahead of its time. It didn’t really work as well as you’d hope, and so it had pretty limited usability, and did not have a successor of note. The virtual reality headsets and glasses, for example, haven’t connected yet. I bet they will at some point though, and the Kinect will be part of their history."
