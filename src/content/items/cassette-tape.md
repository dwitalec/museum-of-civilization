historicalContext: "Developed by Philips in 1963, the compact cassette spent its first decade as a dictation tool before the music industry transformed it into a cultural force. By the late 1970s and through the 1980s, it had overtaken vinyl in sales, and the introduction of the Sony Walkman in 1979 made music truly personal for the first time — something you could carry with you anywhere. But perhaps its most lasting cultural contribution was the mixtape: the ability for ordinary people to sequence and share music, to make something personal out of someone else's art. The cassette democratized both listening and curation in ways that streaming platforms are still building on today."

personalStory: "Cassette tapes – and the radio – defined music for me as a child. The combination of the two was the pinnacle. I remember listening to Casey Kasem’s American Top 40 radio show every week in the 1980’s, and dutifully recording my favorite songs onto a cassette tape so I’d have ready access to them. Yes, I did also brave this technology to laboriously build some classic “mix tapes” as well. This “Spinal Tap” cassette tape, from the 1984 movie, was one of the last two cassette tapes (a “Bangles” tape was the other) I kept as I finally moved on from my collection when we moved in 2014."
