historicalContext: "Amazon launched the first Kindle on November 19, 2007, at $399. It sold out in 5.5 hours and stayed out of stock for months. Jeff Bezos had spent three years quietly building it, convinced that if Amazon was in the business of selling books, it had to reckon with what a digital book could be. The key was e-ink — a display technology that reflected light like paper rather than emitting it, making long reading sessions comfortable in a way no backlit screen had managed. The Kindle didn't just sell e-books; it changed publishing. Authors could bypass traditional gatekeepers, prices dropped, and the industry scrambled to respond. The device itself evolved from a plastic wedge with a physical keyboard to something nearly as thin and light as a paperback. It introduced the idea that a library — thousands of books — could travel in a bag without weight or bulk, and that you could be mid-sentence in one country and finish the chapter in another."

personalStory: "The Amazon Kindle came out the same year as the iPhone, but in many ways it was the opposite of the iPhone. While the iPhone would very quickly launch an app store that enabled it to do almost anything, the Kindle had one purpose -- reading books, simply and in black-and-white. It took me a while to understand the power in this -- the pictured Kindle is from 2013, which I believe is the first time I owned one -- but I've come to appreciate its focus. This is especially true as we have come to understand the dangers of getting side-tracked with social media and other iPhone distractions. Its e-ink really is a thing of wonder too -- with a battery that needs charging only every week or two and a crispness that soothes rather than excites. It's also nice to not have quite so many physical books cluttering the house."
