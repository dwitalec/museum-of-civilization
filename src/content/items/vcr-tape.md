historicalContext: "JVC's VHS format, launched in 1976 and dominant by the mid-1980s after defeating Betamax in one of history's most famous format wars, fundamentally changed the relationship between audiences and media. For the first time, people could watch what they wanted, when they wanted — recording television to watch later (time-shifting) and renting movies to watch at home. Blockbuster Video, which opened its first store in 1985, was built entirely on this possibility. The VCR also introduced the idea of owning a copy of a film, something previously unimaginable for consumers. It was the first crack in the broadcast model of media that streaming would eventually shatter completely."

personalStory: "My first memory of the VCR was my Uncle Bruce telling me about how you could now watch movies of your choice from home whenever you want – a novel concept at the time. When we got our first VCR, I remember scheduling it to record David Letterman episodes, whose 12:30 AM start time had previously been out of reach for me. I also recorded various movies from our “The Movie Channel” cable subscription. Later, we got a video camera and recorded various home movies and sports events via VCR tapes. And much later, in the 2000’s, I would record “The Sopranos” and then bring in the tape to work so my friend Jay could watch it (without getting his own HBO subscription)."
