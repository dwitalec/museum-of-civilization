historicalContext: "The PalmPilot, introduced by Palm Computing in 1996, was the device that made personal digital assistants practical for everyday people. Earlier PDAs had failed by trying to replace a laptop; Palm succeeded by doing less — contacts, calendar, notes, and a to-do list — but doing it instantly and reliably. It introduced Graffiti, a simplified handwriting input system, and its HotSync cradle made syncing with a desktop PC seamless. The PalmPilot sold over a million units in its first year and established the template that every smartphone would eventually follow: a pocket-sized personal computer built around your daily life, not your desk."

personalStory: "I bought my first PalmPilot as a new consultant in the late 1990’s. Keeping track of all of my contacts in one place was a huge benefit for me. Learning the “Graffiti” language to input information in the device with its stylus was also fun and made me feel like I was on the cutting edge. The small section of the screen that was “touch-enabled” felt amazing to me. Just imagine if I was able to call or text any of those contacts I had! The relative easy syncing with my work computer (my only computer at the time) was also a huge leap forward, though I remember that the calendar did not sync with my Lotus Notes work calendar, so it had less value to me. 
The Palm on display is the Palm Vx, which I believe was my second Palm device. I likely bought it around 2000 and used it through about 2005. By then I had moved most of my contacts to my cell phone and so it had less use to me."
