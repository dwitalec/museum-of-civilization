historicalContext: "The 3.5-inch floppy disk, introduced by Sony in 1980 and popularized through the 1980s and 90s, became the dominant removable storage format of its era. At 1.44 megabytes — a figure that once felt enormous — it made software distribution, file sharing, and portable data a reality for everyday people. The floppy was the cloud before the cloud: your work, your games, your files, small enough to carry in a shirt pocket. It defined how a generation thought about data as something physical you could hold, loan, lose, and copy."

personalStory: "The true “floppy” disc was this 3.5” disc’s predecessor, the 5 ¼” version. It flopped as you inserted it into early computers. But since “hard drives” were something else completely, they kept the “floppy” name for this “non-floppy” 3.5” version. It was critical for storing files (like on “Word Perfect” or “Lotus 123”), and I remember buying various cheap computer games on these discs to play on my Adam computer."
